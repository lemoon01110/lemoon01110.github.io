Problem 1: Basic Derivation

Show that if two events are independent, their joint surprisal adds up.

(This problem directly tests your understanding of the concepts defined in the Definitions section.)
Reveal Solution to Problem 1
Solution

Let \(X\) and \(Y\) be independent random variables. By definition: \[ P(X, Y) = P(X)P(Y) \] Taking the negative log base 2 of both sides: \[ \begin{aligned} h(X, Y) &= -\log_2 P(X, Y) \\ &= -\log_2 \big(P(X)P(Y)\big) \\ &= -\log_2 P(X) - \log_2 P(Y) \\ &= h(X) + h(Y) \end{aligned} \] Hence, joint surprisal is additive. \(\blacksquare\)

Problem 2: Applied Metric

Explain why Cross-Entropy is always greater than or equal to Entropy: \(H(P, Q) \ge H(P)\).

(This problem requires applying the mathematical equivalence detailed in the Relations to Other Fields section.)
Reveal Solution to Problem 2
Solution

Recall that the Kullback-Leibler (KL) Divergence represents: \[ D_{\text{KL}}(P \parallel Q) = H(P, Q) - H(P) \] By Gibbs’ Inequality, we know that \(D_{\text{KL}}(P \parallel Q) \ge 0\) for all probability distributions \(P\) and \(Q\). Consequently: \[ H(P, Q) - H(P) \ge 0 \implies H(P, Q) \ge H(P) \] The inequality holds with equality if and only if \(P = Q\). \(\blacksquare\)
