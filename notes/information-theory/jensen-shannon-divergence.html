Jensen-Shannon Divergence

A smoothed, symmetric, and bounded correction to the Kullback-Leibler Divergence.

1. Definitions

Definition: Jensen-Shannon Divergence

Given two probability distributions \(P\) and \(Q\), we first define the mixture distribution \(M\) as their exact point-wise average:

\[ M = \frac{1}{2}(P + Q) \]

The Jensen-Shannon Divergence (JSD) is then defined as the average of the KL divergences from \(P\) to \(M\), and from \(Q\) to \(M\):

\[ JSD(P \| Q) = \frac{1}{2} D_{KL}(P \| M) + \frac{1}{2} D_{KL}(Q \| M) \]

2. Intuitions & Remarks

A Symmetric Fix to KL Divergence

While the Kullback-Leibler (KL) Divergence is foundational, it suffers from two major mathematical flaws that limit its utility as a true distance metric: 1. It is asymmetric (\(D_{KL}(P \| Q) \neq D_{KL}(Q \| P)\)). 2. It can explode to infinity if the distributions do not share the exact same support (i.e., if \(Q(x) = 0\) where \(P(x) > 0\)).

The Jensen-Shannon Divergence solves both of these problems by creating a smoothed, symmetric version. Instead of measuring how far \(Q\) is from \(P\) directly, it measures how far both distributions are from their perfectly balanced “middle ground” mixture \(M\).

Because \(M\) contains the probability mass of both \(P\) and \(Q\), it is impossible for \(M(x)\) to be zero when \(P(x)\) or \(Q(x)\) is greater than zero. This guarantees that the KL divergences to \(M\) never explode to infinity, providing immense mathematical stability.

3. Formulas & Key Properties

Key Properties of JSD

1. Perfect Symmetry

Unlike KL divergence, JSD is perfectly symmetric. It doesn’t matter which distribution is the “reference” and which is the “approximation.” \[ JSD(P \| Q) = JSD(Q \| P) \]

2. Strict Bounds

Unlike KL divergence which stretches from \(0\) to \(\infty\), JSD is strictly bounded between \(0\) and \(\log(2)\) (or \(1\) if using base-2 logarithms). \[ 0 \le JSD(P \| Q) \le \log(2) \] This absolute boundedness provides immense numerical stability during machine learning training, preventing exploding gradients.

3. A True Mathematical Metric

While JSD itself does not satisfy the triangle inequality, its square root actually does! The quantity \(\sqrt{JSD(P \| Q)}\) satisfies non-negativity, symmetry, identity of indiscernibles, and the triangle inequality, making it a true mathematical distance metric.

Machine Learning Context: The Birth of GANs

The Jensen-Shannon Divergence is permanently etched into machine learning history due to its central role in the original Generative Adversarial Network (GAN) paper by Ian Goodfellow (2014).

In a standard GAN, a Generator network \(G\) tries to produce fake data to fool a Discriminator network \(D\), while \(D\) tries to distinguish between true data (\(P_{data}\)) and fake data (\(P_{generator}\)).

Goodfellow mathematically proved that if the discriminator \(D\) is trained to absolute optimality at any given step, the loss function of the generator precisely collapses into minimizing the Jensen-Shannon Divergence between the true data distribution and the generated distribution:

\[ \mathcal{L}_G = 2 \cdot JSD(P_{data} \| P_{generator}) - \log(4) \]

By engaging in this minimax game, the generator implicitly forces its probability distribution to perfectly match the training data, guided by the smoothed, stable gradients of the JSD.

7. Practice Problems

Problem 1: Proof of Boundedness

Prove mathematically that the Jensen-Shannon Divergence (base 2) is strictly bounded above by exactly \(1\) bit: \[ JSD(P \| Q) \le 1 \]
Reveal Solution to Problem 1
Solution

To prove the upper bound, we express JSD using Shannon Entropy \(H(\cdot)\). Let’s expand the definition:

\[ \begin{aligned} JSD(P \| Q) &= \frac{1}{2} \left[ \sum P(x) \log_2 \frac{P(x)}{M(x)} \right] + \frac{1}{2} \left[ \sum Q(x) \log_2 \frac{Q(x)}{M(x)} \right] \\ &= \frac{1}{2} \left[ \sum P(x) \log_2 P(x) - \sum P(x) \log_2 M(x) \right] \\ &\quad + \frac{1}{2} \left[ \sum Q(x) \log_2 Q(x) - \sum Q(x) \log_2 M(x) \right] \end{aligned} \]

Substitute the definition of Shannon Entropy \(H(P) = -\sum P(x) \log_2 P(x)\):

\[ \begin{aligned} JSD(P \| Q) &= \frac{1}{2} \left[ -H(P) - \sum P(x) \log_2 M(x) \right] + \frac{1}{2} \left[ -H(Q) - \sum Q(x) \log_2 M(x) \right] \\ &= -\frac{1}{2} H(P) - \frac{1}{2} H(Q) - \frac{1}{2} \sum \Big( P(x) + Q(x) \Big) \log_2 M(x) \end{aligned} \]

Recall that \(M(x) = \frac{P(x) + Q(x)}{2}\), meaning \(P(x) + Q(x) = 2M(x)\). Substitute this:

\[ \begin{aligned} JSD(P \| Q) &= -\frac{1}{2} H(P) - \frac{1}{2} H(Q) - \frac{1}{2} \sum \Big( 2M(x) \Big) \log_2 M(x) \\ &= -\frac{1}{2} H(P) - \frac{1}{2} H(Q) - \sum M(x) \log_2 M(x) \end{aligned} \]

Notice that \(-\sum M(x) \log_2 M(x)\) is exactly the entropy of the mixture distribution \(H(M)\). Therefore:

\[ JSD(P \| Q) = H(M) - \frac{H(P) + H(Q)}{2} \]

Because entropy \(H\) is a strictly concave function, the entropy of a mixture \(M\) is bounded above by applying Jensen’s Inequality: \[ H(M) = H\left(\frac{P+Q}{2}\right) \le \frac{1}{2} H(P) + \frac{1}{2} H(Q) + 1 \] (Note: the \(+1\) comes from the bound of a binary mixture in base 2).

Rearranging this inequality gives: \[ H(M) - \left( \frac{H(P) + H(Q)}{2} \right) \le 1 \]

Since the left side is precisely the \(JSD(P \| Q)\), we arrive at our absolute upper bound: \[ JSD(P \| Q) \le 1 \text{ bit} \]

The equality holds if and only if \(P\) and \(Q\) have completely disjoint supports. \(\blacksquare\)

Problem 2: GAN Stabilization

Explain intuitively why this strict boundedness (\(0 \le JSD \le 1\)) makes Jensen-Shannon Divergence vastly superior to KL divergence for training early-stage Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs).
Reveal Solution to Problem 2
Solution

In the early stages of training a GAN, the Generator network is terrible. It produces random noise that looks absolutely nothing like the real data. Mathematically, this means the generated distribution \(P_{generator}\) and the real data distribution \(P_{data}\) have completely non-overlapping (disjoint) supports in the high-dimensional image space.

If you tried to minimize the Forward KL Divergence \(D_{KL}(P_{data} \| P_{generator})\), the ratio \(\frac{P_{data}(x)}{P_{generator}(x)}\) would divide by zero wherever the generator failed to place probability mass. The loss would literally explode to infinity, completely destroying the neural network’s gradients.

However, the JSD explicitly handles disjoint supports gracefully. If the distributions don’t overlap, the JSD simply saturates to its maximum bound of exactly \(\log(2)\) nats (or \(1\) bit). The loss remains a finite, well-behaved number, and the gradient flow is preserved so the Generator can continue learning to bridge the gap.
