Kullback-Leibler Divergence

Quantifying the exact amount of information lost when an approximation is used instead of the truth.

1. Definitions

Definition: KL Divergence (Relative Entropy)

For discrete probability distributions \(P\) and \(Q\) defined on the same probability space \(\mathcal{X}\), the Kullback-Leibler (KL) Divergence from \(Q\) to \(P\) is defined as:

\[ D_{KL}(P \| Q) = \sum_{x \in \mathcal{X}} P(x) \log_2 \left( \frac{P(x)}{Q(x)} \right) \]

For continuous distributions with probability density functions \(p\) and \(q\), the sum becomes an integral: \[ D_{KL}(P \| Q) = \int_{\mathcal{X}} p(x) \ln \left( \frac{p(x)}{q(x)} \right) dx \]

(Note: The double vertical bars \(\|\) emphasize that this is a divergence between two distributions, not a conditional probability. It is read as “The KL divergence of P with respect to Q” or “The KL divergence from Q to P”.)

2. Intuitions & Remarks

The Intuition of Information Loss

If \(P\) represents the “true” underlying distribution of data in nature, and \(Q\) represents our mathematical model’s approximation of that distribution, then \(D_{KL}(P \| Q)\) quantifies the exact amount of information lost when we use \(Q\) instead of \(P\).

Imagine you are trying to compress data using a codebook. If you know the true distribution \(P\), you can build an optimal codebook. If you are forced to use a codebook optimized for \(Q\) instead, your compressed files will be slightly larger. The KL divergence is exactly the number of extra bits required due to using the wrong codebook.

3. Formulas & Key Properties

Key Properties of KL Divergence

1. Gibbs’ Inequality (Non-negativity)

KL Divergence is strictly non-negative. You cannot have “negative” information loss. \[ D_{KL}(P \| Q) \ge 0 \] Equality \(D_{KL}(P \| Q) = 0\) holds if and only if \(P\) and \(Q\) are identical almost everywhere.

2. Lack of Symmetry

KL Divergence is not a true mathematical distance metric because it is highly asymmetric: \[ D_{KL}(P \| Q) \neq D_{KL}(Q \| P) \] This asymmetry has profound implications in machine learning, dictating whether models exhibit “mode-seeking” or “mean-seeking” behavior.

3. Lack of Triangle Inequality

It does not satisfy the triangle inequality. You cannot compute the divergence \(D_{KL}(P \| R)\) simply by bridging through an intermediate distribution \(Q\). Thus, we call it a “divergence” rather than a “distance”.

4. Graphs & Visualizations

Because of the asymmetry, the order of \(P\) and \(Q\) drastically alters the geometric behavior of the approximation when we use KL Divergence as an optimization objective.

Forward KL: \(D_{KL}(P \| Q)\)

  • Penalty: If \(P(x) > 0\) but \(Q(x) \to 0\), the ratio \(\frac{P(x)}{Q(x)}\) explodes to infinity. Therefore, \(Q\) is forced to place probability mass everywhere \(P\) places mass.
  • Result: \(Q\) stretches out to “cover” all the modes (peaks) of \(P\), resulting in a broad, mean-seeking approximation.

Reverse KL: \(D_{KL}(Q \| P)\)

  • Penalty: If \(Q(x) > 0\) but \(P(x) \to 0\), the ratio \(\frac{Q(x)}{P(x)}\) explodes. Therefore, \(Q\) must absolutely avoid placing mass where \(P\) has no mass.
  • Result: \(Q\) tends to “collapse” into a single valid peak (mode) of \(P\) to safely avoid areas where \(P=0\). This results in a highly focused, mode-seeking approximation.

5. Relations to Other Fields

Field Interpretation / Mapping Application Example
Variational Inference We approximate complex posteriors with simple networks by explicitly minimizing the Reverse KL Divergence. Variational Autoencoders (VAEs)
Reinforcement Learning We add a KL penalty to ensure newly updated policies do not diverge too wildly from old policies. Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO)
Information Bottleneck Networks try to maximize mutual information with targets while minimizing KL divergence with inputs. Deep Learning Theory

7. Practice Problems

Problem 1: Proof of Asymmetry

Provide a concrete, numerical proof that \(D_{KL}(P \| Q) \neq D_{KL}(Q \| P)\) using two simple discrete probability distributions: \(P=[0.5, 0.5]\) and \(Q=[0.9, 0.1]\).
Reveal Solution to Problem 1
Solution

Let \(P = [0.5, 0.5]\) and \(Q = [0.9, 0.1]\).

Forward KL Divergence: \[ \begin{aligned} D_{KL}(P \| Q) &= \sum P(x) \log_2 \left( \frac{P(x)}{Q(x)} \right) \\ &= 0.5 \log_2\left(\frac{0.5}{0.9}\right) + 0.5 \log_2\left(\frac{0.5}{0.1}\right) \\ &= 0.5 \log_2(0.555) + 0.5 \log_2(5) \\ &\approx 0.5(-0.849) + 0.5(2.321) \\ &\approx -0.424 + 1.161 \\ &= 0.737 \text{ bits} \end{aligned} \]

Reverse KL Divergence: \[ \begin{aligned} D_{KL}(Q \| P) &= \sum Q(x) \log_2 \left( \frac{Q(x)}{P(x)} \right) \\ &= 0.9 \log_2\left(\frac{0.9}{0.5}\right) + 0.1 \log_2\left(\frac{0.1}{0.5}\right) \\ &= 0.9 \log_2(1.8) + 0.1 \log_2(0.2) \\ &\approx 0.9(0.848) + 0.1(-2.321) \\ &\approx 0.763 - 0.232 \\ &= 0.531 \text{ bits} \end{aligned} \]

Since \(0.737 \neq 0.531\), we have proven that the KL divergence is fundamentally asymmetric. \(\blacksquare\)

Problem 2: Gibbs’ Inequality

Use Jensen’s Inequality to mathematically prove Gibbs’ Inequality: \(D_{KL}(P \| Q) \ge 0\). (Hint: Remember that Jensen’s Inequality states that for any strictly concave function like \(\ln(x)\), the expected value of the function is less than or equal to the function of the expected value: \(\mathbb{E}[\ln(X)] \le \ln(\mathbb{E}[X])\)).
Reveal Solution to Problem 2
Solution

Let’s begin with the definition of KL Divergence using the natural logarithm: \[ D_{KL}(P \| Q) = \sum_{x} P(x) \ln \left( \frac{P(x)}{Q(x)} \right) \]

We can rewrite the ratio inside the logarithm by negating it: \[ D_{KL}(P \| Q) = -\sum_{x} P(x) \ln \left( \frac{Q(x)}{P(x)} \right) \]

Notice that \(\sum_{x} P(x) [\dots]\) is exactly the definition of the mathematical expectation \(\mathbb{E}_{X \sim P}[\dots]\). Thus: \[ D_{KL}(P \| Q) = -\mathbb{E}_{X \sim P} \left[ \ln \left( \frac{Q(X)}{P(X)} \right) \right] \]

Since the natural logarithm function \(\ln(z)\) is strictly concave, we can apply Jensen’s Inequality, which states that \(-\mathbb{E}[\ln(Z)] \ge -\ln(\mathbb{E}[Z])\): \[ -\mathbb{E}_{X \sim P} \left[ \ln \left( \frac{Q(X)}{P(X)} \right) \right] \ge -\ln \left( \mathbb{E}_{X \sim P} \left[ \frac{Q(X)}{P(X)} \right] \right) \]

Now, let’s evaluate the inner expectation: \[ \mathbb{E}_{X \sim P} \left[ \frac{Q(X)}{P(X)} \right] = \sum_{x} P(x) \frac{Q(x)}{P(x)} = \sum_{x} Q(x) = 1 \] Because \(Q\) is a valid probability distribution, its probabilities must sum to 1.

Substituting this back into the inequality: \[ D_{KL}(P \| Q) \ge -\ln(1) = 0 \]

Therefore, \(D_{KL}(P \| Q) \ge 0\). \(\blacksquare\)
