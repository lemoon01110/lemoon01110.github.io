Information Content (Surprisal)

Understanding the foundational building block of Information Theory: measuring the “surprise” of an event.

1. Definitions

Every concept in Information Theory stems from quantifying how much information we gain when an event occurs.

Definition: Information Content (Surprisal)

For a discrete random variable \(X\) with a probability mass function \(P(X)\), the information content (or surprisal) of a specific event \(x\) is defined as the negative logarithm of its probability:

\[ h(x) = -\log_b P(x) = \log_b \left( \frac{1}{P(x)} \right) \]

where:

  • \(P(x)\) is the prior probability of the event occurring.
  • \(b\) is the base of the logarithm, which determines the unit of information:
    • Base 2 (\(b=2\)): Measured in bits (or shannons). This is the standard in computer science. One bit represents the information gained from a single fair coin flip.
    • Base \(e\) (\(b=e\)): Measured in nats. Commonly used in machine learning because the natural logarithm is easier to differentiate.
    • Base 10 (\(b=10\)): Measured in bans (or hartleys), though this is rarely used in modern contexts.

2. Intuitions & Remarks

The Intuition of Surprise

The core intuition relies on a simple premise: > Observing a highly probable event provides very little information, whereas observing a rare or highly improbable event provides a massive amount of information.

If someone tells you, “The sun rose this morning,” you have gained almost zero information because the probability of that event is near \(1\). Conversely, if someone tells you, “It is currently snowing in the Sahara Desert,” you have gained a tremendous amount of information because the prior probability of that event is near \(0\).

3. Formulas & Key Properties

The definition \(h(x) = -\log P(x)\) was specifically chosen by Claude Shannon because it perfectly satisfies three necessary axioms of information:

Axioms of Information

1. Non-negativity

Information cannot be negative. You cannot “lose” information by observing an event. \[ P(x) \in [0, 1] \implies h(x) \ge 0 \]

2. Zero Surprise for Certainty

If an event is guaranteed to happen (\(P(x) = 1\)), observing it yields no new information. \[ h(x) = -\log(1) = 0 \]

3. Joint Additivity

If two events \(x\) and \(y\) are statistically independent, the joint probability is the product of their individual probabilities. Consequently, the total information gained from observing both events is the sum of the information gained from observing them individually.

To see the exact mathematical derivation of this property, scroll down to Practice Problem 1.

5. Relations to Other Fields

Field Interpretation / Mapping Application Example
Deep Learning Minimizing surprisal is equivalent to minimizing the Negative Log-Likelihood (NLL). By minimizing this loss, a neural network learns to be “less surprised” by the actual ground-truth data. Classification training objectives
Computer Science The minimum number of bits required to encode an unexpected message perfectly. Data Compression (Huffman Coding)

7. Practice Problems

Problem 1: Additivity of Independent Events

Show mathematically that if two events are statistically independent, their joint surprisal adds up (\(h(X, Y) = h(X) + h(Y)\)).
Reveal Solution to Problem 1
Solution

Let \(X\) and \(Y\) be independent random variables. By the definition of independence, their joint probability is the product of their marginals: \[ P(X, Y) = P(X)P(Y) \] Taking the negative log base 2 of both sides: \[ \begin{aligned} h(X, Y) &= -\log_2 P(X, Y) \\ &= -\log_2 \big(P(X)P(Y)\big) \\ &= -\log_2 P(X) - \log_2 P(Y) \\ &= h(X) + h(Y) \end{aligned} \] Hence, joint surprisal is additive. \(\blacksquare\)

Problem 2: Calculating Surprisal

Calculate the surprisal of rolling a 6 on a fair 6-sided die, in both bits and nats. How does this compare to the surprisal of flipping two heads in a row on a fair coin?
Reveal Solution to Problem 2
Solution

Die Roll: The probability of rolling a 6 is \(P(x) = \frac{1}{6}\). In bits (base 2): \[ h(x) = -\log_2\left(\frac{1}{6}\right) = \log_2(6) \approx 2.585 \text{ bits} \] In nats (base \(e\)): \[ h(x) = -\ln\left(\frac{1}{6}\right) = \ln(6) \approx 1.792 \text{ nats} \]

Coin Flips: The probability of flipping two heads in a row is \(P(y) = \frac{1}{4}\). In bits: \[ h(y) = -\log_2\left(\frac{1}{4}\right) = \log_2(4) = 2 \text{ bits} \]

Rolling a 6 provides more surprise (2.585 bits) than flipping two heads in a row (2 bits) because \(\frac{1}{6}\) is less probable than \(\frac{1}{4}\).
