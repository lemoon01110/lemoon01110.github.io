Mutual Information

Understanding Mutual Information

Mutual Information (MI) quantifies the amount of information obtained about one random variable by observing another random variable. Intuitively, it measures how much knowing \(Y\) reduces the uncertainty about \(X\).

If \(X\) and \(Y\) are completely independent, knowing \(Y\) tells you absolutely nothing about \(X\), and their mutual information is zero. If \(X\) is a deterministic function of \(Y\), knowing \(Y\) tells you everything about \(X\), and their mutual information is maximized.

Unlike correlation, which only captures linear dependencies, Mutual Information captures all non-linear dependencies between variables.

Mathematical Definition

For two discrete random variables \(X\) and \(Y\) with a joint probability mass function \(P(X, Y)\) and marginals \(P(X)\) and \(P(Y)\), Mutual Information \(I(X;Y)\) is defined as:

\[ I(X;Y) = \sum_{y \in Y} \sum_{x \in X} P(x, y) \log \left( \frac{P(x, y)}{P(x)P(y)} \right) \]

For continuous variables, the summations are replaced by double integrals: \[ I(X;Y) = \int_{\mathcal{Y}} \int_{\mathcal{X}} p(x, y) \log \left( \frac{p(x, y)}{p(x)p(y)} \right) dx dy \]

Relationships to Entropy and Divergence

Mutual Information has beautiful, symmetric relationships with Shannon Entropy and Kullback-Leibler (KL) Divergence.

1. The Entropy Perspective

We can rewrite MI in terms of Marginal and Conditional Entropies:

\[ \begin{aligned} I(X;Y) &= H(X) - H(X|Y) \\ &= H(Y) - H(Y|X) \\ &= H(X) + H(Y) - H(X,Y) \\ &= H(X,Y) - H(X|Y) - H(Y|X) \end{aligned} \]

The first equation, \(H(X) - H(X|Y)\), perfectly matches the intuition: it is the total uncertainty in \(X\), minus the remaining uncertainty in \(X\) after we have observed \(Y\).

2. The KL Divergence Perspective

Notice that the term inside the log of the definition is the ratio between the joint distribution \(P(X, Y)\) and the product of the marginals \(P(X)P(Y)\).

Therefore, Mutual Information is precisely the Kullback-Leibler Divergence between the true joint distribution and the joint distribution assuming \(X\) and \(Y\) are independent:

\[ I(X;Y) = D_{KL} \Big( P(X,Y) \Big\| P(X)P(Y) \Big) \]

Because KL Divergence is always non-negative, \(I(X;Y) \ge 0\), with equality if and only if \(X\) and \(Y\) are strictly independent.

Machine Learning Context

Mutual Information is heavily utilized across modern ML and Representation Learning paradigms:

1. InfoGAN

Standard GANs suffer from highly entangled latent spaces. InfoGAN solves this by maximizing the Mutual Information \(I(c; G(z, c))\) between a subset of latent variables \(c\) and the generated image \(G\). This forces the network to learn disentangled, interpretable representations (e.g., allocating one dimension of \(c\) to rotation, another to lighting).

2. Feature Selection

In classical ML, estimating \(I(Feature; Label)\) is a powerful method for filtering features. Features with high mutual information regarding the target variable are retained, while those with near-zero MI are pruned, handling complex non-linear correlations that ANOVA or Pearson correlation would miss.

3. Contrastive Predictive Coding (CPC)

In self-supervised learning, calculating exact mutual information is often intractable in high dimensions. Methods like InfoNCE provide a lower bound on MI, allowing networks to learn robust representations by maximizing the mutual information between different “views” (e.g., augmented crops) of the same image.
