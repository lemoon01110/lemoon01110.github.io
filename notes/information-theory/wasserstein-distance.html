Wasserstein Distance

Optimal Transport: Minimizing the physical work required to reshape one distribution into another.

1. Definitions

Definition: Wasserstein Distance

For two probability distributions \(P\) and \(Q\), the \(1^{st}\) Wasserstein Distance \(W_1\) is defined as the infimum (minimum) expected cost of transporting mass from \(P\) to \(Q\):

\[ W_1(P, Q) = \inf_{\gamma \in \Pi(P, Q)} \mathbb{E}_{(x, y) \sim \gamma} \big[ \|x - y\| \big] \]

Breaking down the math:

  1. \(\Pi(P, Q)\): The set of all possible “transport plans”. A joint distribution plan \(\gamma(x, y)\) dictates exactly how much mass to move from location \(x\) to location \(y\). To be a valid plan, its marginals must equal \(P\) and \(Q\).
  2. \(\|x - y\|\): The physical distance between location \(x\) and \(y\) in the underlying metric space.
  3. \(\mathbb{E}\): The expected cost (work) of executing the transport plan.
  4. \(\inf\): The infimum. We are searching for the single optimal transport plan that requires the absolute least amount of work.

2. Intuitions & Remarks

The Earth Mover’s Distance (EMD)

The Wasserstein Distance is intuitively known as the Earth Mover’s Distance.

Imagine probability distributions as physical piles of dirt (Earth). The Wasserstein distance asks: What is the absolute minimum amount of “work” required to physically shovel the dirt from distribution \(P\) until it perfectly matches the shape of distribution \(Q\)?

Work in physics is defined as: Amount of dirt moved \(\times\) Distance moved.

Because it explicitly measures the physical distance the mass must travel, the Wasserstein distance provides a smooth, meaningful value (and crucially, a non-zero gradient) even when the distributions are miles apart and do not overlap at all.

3. Formulas & Key Properties

Overcoming the Limits of Divergences

Both the Kullback-Leibler (KL) and Jensen-Shannon (JSD) divergences are fundamentally based on the ratio of probabilities \(P(x) / Q(x)\) at exactly the same spatial location \(x\).

This mathematical foundation creates a severe problem: if the two distributions have disjoint supports (meaning they don’t overlap in space), these divergences either become infinite or saturate at a constant value.

When divergences saturate, their gradients become exactly zero. In Machine Learning, if your loss function has zero gradient, your neural network receives no signal and cannot learn.

Wasserstein distance measures the horizontal cost of moving the mass, rather than just the vertical ratio at a fixed location, completely bypassing this issue.

5. Relations to Other Fields

Machine Learning Context: WGANs and Duality

The mathematical definition of \(W_1(P, Q)\) is beautiful, but computationally intractable to calculate directly because searching over all possible joint distributions \(\Pi(P, Q)\) is impossible for high-dimensional data like images.

In 2017, the landmark paper Wasserstein GAN solved this by applying the Kantorovich-Rubinstein Duality. The duality theorem states that instead of searching for the optimal transport plan, you can calculate the Wasserstein distance by finding a 1-Lipschitz continuous function \(f\) that maximizes the difference in expectations:

\[ W_1(P, Q) = \sup_{\|f\|_L \le 1} \left( \mathbb{E}_{x \sim P}[f(x)] - \mathbb{E}_{y \sim Q}[f(y)] \right) \]

In a WGAN, the Discriminator (now called a “Critic”) is simply a neural network parameterized to represent \(f(x)\). To enforce the \(1\)-Lipschitz constraint (\(\|f\|_L \le 1\)), modern implementations use Gradient Penalty (WGAN-GP), ensuring the gradients of the critic respect a norm of \(1\). WGANs completely eliminate the vanishing gradient problem, resulting in vastly more stable training.

7. Practice Problems

Problem 1: Disjoint Supports and Gradients

Explain mathematically why the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence provides a zero gradient when two distributions are completely disjoint, but the Wasserstein distance provides a highly useful constant gradient.
Reveal Solution to Problem 1
Solution

Let \(P\) be the true data distribution (a spike at \(x=0\)) and \(Q_\theta\) be our parameterized model distribution (a spike at \(x=\theta\)). Suppose they are currently disjoint (\(\theta > 0\)).

For KL Divergence: The divergence is defined by the point-wise ratio at exactly the same spatial location \(x\): \[ D_{KL}(P \| Q_\theta) = \sum P(x) \log \left( \frac{P(x)}{Q_\theta(x)} \right) \] Since \(P\) is only non-zero at \(x=0\), we only evaluate the ratio at \(x=0\). However, \(Q_\theta(0) = 0\) because \(Q_\theta\) is a spike at \(\theta\). Thus, \(D_{KL} = 1 \cdot \log\left(\frac{1}{0}\right) = \infty\).

Because it is \(\infty\) for all \(\theta > 0\), the derivative with respect to \(\theta\) is exactly \(0\). The loss landscape is a flat, infinite plateau, meaning the network has absolutely no idea which direction to move \(\theta\) to find the true data.

For Wasserstein Distance: The Earth Mover’s distance measures the physical work to transport the spike of mass from \(x=\theta\) to \(x=0\). Since the total mass is \(1\), the work is simply: \[ W_1(P, Q_\theta) = 1 \cdot \|\theta - 0\| = |\theta| \] The gradient with respect to \(\theta\) (for \(\theta > 0\)) is exactly \(1\).

The loss landscape is a perfect V-shape \(f(\theta) = |\theta|\). This gives a beautiful, constant, non-zero gradient pointing directly toward the true data, allowing the neural network to effortlessly slide \(\theta\) down to \(0\) via gradient descent. \(\blacksquare\)
